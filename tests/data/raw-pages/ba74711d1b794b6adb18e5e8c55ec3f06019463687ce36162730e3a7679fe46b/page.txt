We must adhere to Wikipedia's policy of Neutral Point of View. While it is a fact that the film is widely considered racist propaganda, the article itself, particularly the lead sentence, should reflect what reliable sources say about the film, not present a single viewpoint as an absolute, un-nuanced fact. A more encyclopedic approach would be to state that the film is widely regarded as one of the most controversial and racist films ever made, and to explain its propagandistic nature and themes, including its glorification of the Ku Klux Klan and lynching. This approach avoids violating NPOV while still being direct and accurate. The addition of "white supremacist propaganda" in the opening sentence is not neutral and the emphasis on it's placement would 'appear' to be coming from a non-neutral space more concerned with promoting this view point, which will not be universal, than presenting the article content, which is the purpose of the lead and main reason for immediate identification in the opening sentence. The comparison to Triumph of the Will is not a valid argument for changing this article. Each article on Wikipedia must stand on its own and follow the guidelines. We must not violate NPOV in one article simply because another may be seen as doing so. [[User:Darkwarriorblake|Darkwarriorblake]] ([[User talk:Darkwarriorblake|talk]]) 14:29, 15 August 2025 (UTC)

I might note that the ''Britannica'' entry has a lead consisting of a single paragraph, which calls the film racist in the equivalent of [[WP:WikiVoice]] ({{tq|[the film] has long been hailed for its technical and dramatic innovations. It does not seem like a terribly tall order to introduce both parts of the complicated legacy—i.e. both the positive aspect in terms of filmmaking and the negative aspect in terms of racism—in the first paragraph, while leaving the details for later paragraphs.  '''''The Birth of a Nation''''' is a 1915 American [[Silent film|silent]] [[Epic film|epic]] [[Drama (film and television)|drama film]] directed by [[D. W. Griffith]] and starring [[Lillian Gish]]. It is an adaptation of [[Thomas Dixon Jr.]]'s 1905 novel and play ''[[The Clansman: A Historical Romance of the Ku Klux Klan]]'', co-written by Griffith and [[Frank E. Woods]] and produced by Griffith and [[Harry Aitken]]. Historically, it is recognized for its role in the development of cinematic technique, with innovative use of camera shots and editing. Culturally, it is noted for its overtly racist content, which includes demeaning portrayals of African Americans and a positive depiction of the [[Ku Klux Klan]].

 The film's plot, a blend of historical events and fiction, chronicles the [[assassination of Abraham Lincoln]] and the relationship between two families—the pro-[[Union (American Civil War)|Union]] Stonemans and the pro-[[Confederate States of America|Confederacy]] Camerons—during the [[American Civil War|Civil War]] and [[Reconstruction era|Reconstruction]] eras. ''The Birth of a Nation'' was the first [[Serial film|non-serial]] American 12-[[Film reel|reel]] film and was screened inside the White House for President [[Woodrow Wilson]]. It also helped to pioneer [[close-up|closeup]]s and [[Fade (filmmaking)|fadeouts]], and it includes a carefully staged battle sequence with hundreds of [[extra (acting)|extra]]s made to look like thousands. 

 Upon its release, the film was a massive commercial success, grossing more than any previous motion picture and remaining one of the highest-grossing films ever made when adjusted for inflation. This success was both a consequence and a contributor to the prevailing racial segregation in the United States. In response to its content and its depictions of black people—including the use of white actors in blackface and negative stereotypes—African Americans across the U.S. organized protests. The [[NAACP]] and black leaders in various localities spearheaded campaigns to have the film banned, citing its potential to inflame racial tensions and incite violence. As a result, its release was denied in several cities and the state of Ohio. Griffith's indignation at these censorship efforts motivated him to produce his next film, ''[[Intolerance (film)|Intolerance]]'' (1916).

 The film has been acknowledged as an inspiration for [[Ku Klux Klan#Second Klan: 1915–1944|the revival of the Ku Klux Klan]] in the early 20th century, which occurred shortly after its release.
