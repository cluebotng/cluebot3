== Eigenstates ==

It is a simple matter to find the eigenvalues Sqrt[1-b)/(1+b)] and its inverse, where b is beta, and eigenstates ([1, 1] and [-1, 1]) of the [x, ct] Lorentz transformation. Some commentators on web forums claim that the eigenvalues are related to the [[Relativistic Doppler effect|Doppler effect]]. Should this be discussed in the article? [[User:Xxanthippe|Xxanthippe]] ([[User talk:Xxanthippe|talk]]) 22:52, 10 July 2024 (UTC).

:Yes, a photon moving in the same direction as the implicit boost direction of the Lorentz transformation will have its energy and momentum scaled by the same multiplicative factor — an amount determined by the Doppler shift.  Because it is the same scaling factor for the momentum and energy, that's saying that the photon's four-momentum is an eigenstate.  Likewise for a photon with the opposite momentum — that is, with momentum antiparallel to the implicit boost direction of the Lorentz transformation — it will be red-shifted rather than blue-shifted but otherwise this too is a Doppler shift and an eigenstate.  If we do mention any of this in the article, I'm thinking it should be exceedingly brief.  —[[User:Quantling|<span class="texhtml"><i>Q</i></span>uantling]]&nbsp;([[User talk:Quantling|talk]]&nbsp;&#124;&nbsp;[[Special:Contributions/Quantling|contribs]]) 15:33, 11 July 2024 (UTC)
::The eigenstructure is a significant algebraic feature of any linear transformation, so it is odd that it is not mentioned in an article on the most important linear transformation in physics. This case, in particular, needs some discussion as it involves the apparent paradox (to Galilean thinkers) of transforming between frames that are both traveling at the same speed c, and the limiting processes that are involved because of that. However, I will leave additions to this topic to editors with more experience than myself. [[User:Xxanthippe|Xxanthippe]] ([[User talk:Xxanthippe|talk]]) 04:23, 12 July 2024 (UTC).
:More of a technical question, but how does the eigenvector interpretation generalise to higher dimensions? For example, if we have two dimensions (space and time), we find two linearly independent eigenvectors whose direction (speed) does not change, c and -c. So far so good. However, if we add another spatial dimension, introducing the famous light cones, what would the independent eigenvectors be in this case? As far as I understand, an n-dimensional space can only have n linearly-independent eigenvectors (in this case n=3), which does not seem to be enough to represent the vectors where the speed does not change (light cones). [[User:Viktaur|Viktaur]] ([[User talk:Viktaur|talk]]) 22:36, 13 August 2024 (UTC)

==Galilean and Lorentz transformations==
In the section on '''Physical implications''' it is claimed that ''For relative speeds much less than the speed of light, the Lorentz transformations reduce to the Galilean transformation''. This seems questionable. As <math>\beta</math> (''= v/c'') approaches zero, the leading terms in the Lorentz transformation ''L'' with one spatial dimension {''ct, x''} become 

''L'' =<math display="block">
\begin{bmatrix}
  \ 1& -\beta\\
  -\beta & 1\\
  \end{bmatrix}</math>.

However, the [[Galilean invariance|Galilean transformation]] ''G'', where time is taken to be a universal invariant is, purportedly valid for all <math>\beta</math>, 

''G'' =<math display="block">
\begin{bmatrix}
  \ 1& 0\\
  -\beta & 1\\
  \end{bmatrix}</math>,
which is qualitatively different. Should the statement be removed? [[User:Xxanthippe|Xxanthippe]] ([[User talk:Xxanthippe|talk]]) 05:50, 28 November 2024 (UTC).
:Done. [[User:Xxanthippe|Xxanthippe]] ([[User talk:Xxanthippe|talk]]) 03:26, 3 December 2024 (UTC).

:: Yet, it is well-known to be correct. Note that 
:::<math display="block">
L = \begin{bmatrix}
  \ \gamma & - \gamma \beta \\
  -\gamma \beta & \gamma\\
  \end{bmatrix},</math>
:: and the time variables have an extra ''c'' factor, so with <math>\gamma</math> very close to 1, this recuces first to
:::<math display="block">c t' = c t - \beta x,</math>
::which for small <math>\beta</math>, dividing by ''c'', in turn recudes to
:::<math display="block"> t' = t.</math>

(NOTE:  Not an editor, not a Special Relativity expert, merely an "educated layman" who was surprised by this.  Am I misunderstanding something?  Thanks.) [[User:Dudley Brooks|Dudley Brooks]] ([[User talk:Dudley Brooks|talk]]) 21:38, 8 July 2025 (UTC)
:A link to [[spacetime interval]] has been inserted into the text. Actually, the link is a [[WP:Redirect]] to a section of [[Spacetime]]. Language in this matter is challenged by tendency in some works to use Metric for quantification of spacetime separations. Metric is unsuitable since the mathematical notion refers to a non-negative quantity, yet in spacetime the separation is sometimes negative. Further, as you note the squares are involved and the Euclidean precedent calls for a square root, something not done in spacetime since square roots of negative numbers are imaginary, a notion to be avoided here. Thank you for noting the subtlety; the new link aims to clarify the matter. — [[User:Rgdboer|Rgdboer]] ([[User talk:Rgdboer|talk]]) 23:22, 8 July 2025 (UTC)
:: As Rgdboer says, the terminology in use in the field is problematic, with "metric" and "interval" being used in incompatible ways by different people.
