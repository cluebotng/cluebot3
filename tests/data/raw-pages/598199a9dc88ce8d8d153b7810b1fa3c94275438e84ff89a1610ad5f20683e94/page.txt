== Mention of US shared weapons in the UK in the lead ==

The article currently reads:

In addition, six non-nuclear-armed states currently have [[nuclear weapons sharing|foreign nuclear weapons based on their territory]]. United States weapons are deployed in [[Belgium and weapons of mass destruction|Belgium]], [[Germany and weapons of mass destruction|Germany]], [[Italy and weapons of mass destruction|Italy]], [[Netherlands and weapons of mass destruction|the Netherlands]], and [[Turkey and weapons of mass destruction|Turkey]], while Russian weapons are deployed in Belarus. During the Cold War, NATO and Soviet nuclear weapons were deployed in at least 23 countries.

I suggest it reads:

In addition, six non-nuclear-armed states currently have [[nuclear weapons sharing|foreign nuclear weapons based on their territory]]. United States weapons are deployed in [[Belgium and weapons of mass destruction|Belgium]], [[Germany and weapons of mass destruction|Germany]], [[Italy and weapons of mass destruction|Italy]], [[Netherlands and weapons of mass destruction|the Netherlands]], and [[Turkey and weapons of mass destruction|Turkey]], while Russian weapons are deployed in Belarus. The US also deploys weapons in the United Kingdom. During the Cold War, NATO and Soviet nuclear weapons were deployed in at least 23 countries.

I believe despite the qualification "non-nuclear-armed states" in the first line, many new readers will incorrectly assume that list of foreign US deployments is comprehensive. By not discussing nuclear sharing among nuclear states, the lead could also create some strange opposite perception for new readers, like that US nuclear weapons are widely shared to not only the UK but France or others. 

This section of the lead should also continue to make clear shared weapons come with no sharing of authority, so the UK's status is changed by their presence. [[User:Doeze|Doeze]] ([[User talk:Doeze|talk]]) 15:25, 4 August 2025 (UTC)

== Order of acquisition ==

The lead currently reads "In order of acquisition of nuclear weapons, these are ..." and lists India before Pakistan, based on the 1974 Indian Smiling Buddha and 1998 Pakistani Chagai tests. But the Smiling Buddha device arguably fits the definition of a "nuclear device" rather than a usable weapon, and India only developed weaponized designs in 1994 per https://doi.org/10.1515%2F9780691223063-005. Pakistan on the other hand received extensive help from China, allegedly developing a HEU bomb in 1984 per https://web.archive.org/web/20140810150255/http://www.thenews.com.pk/Todays-News-9-252241-A-historic-day and more importantly in 1990 having China test a Pakistani-built derivative bomb of an early Chinese design https://doi.org/10.1063%2F1.2982122. Obviously "weaponization" can be a bit difficult to qualify, and many details of these programs remain unclear, but we should consider a details note if not changing the order. [[User:Doeze|Doeze]] ([[User talk:Doeze|talk]]) 23:36, 17 August 2025 (UTC)
:I don't think anyone credits India's 1974 peaceful nuclear explosion as anything other than a cover story for a nuclear weapon test.  The distinction is not particularly meaningful.  And Pakistan's nuclear weapons program was clearly prompted by India's test.  So the current sequence makes sense.  My impression is that the article uses order of overt testing as a proxy for order of acquisition, since in most cases they coincide.  Pakistan and Israel are exceptions.  Pakistan tested overtly about a decade after it likely developed a bomb and Israel never overtly tested or confirmed acquisition. [[User:NPguy|NPguy]] ([[User talk:NPguy|talk]]) 00:28, 18 August 2025 (UTC)
